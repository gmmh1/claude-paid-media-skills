## Purpose
Provide the reusable persuasion-structure knowledge base — which framework fits which situation, and how to apply it — that every platform-specific copywriting skill in this library draws on rather than reinventing.

## Trigger Conditions
- "What copywriting framework should I use for this ad"
- "Apply AIDA / PAS / BAB to this product"
- "Which persuasion angle fits this message"

## Required Inputs
- Product/service and core offer
- Audience awareness stage (unaware, problem-aware, solution-aware, product-aware, most-aware — Eugene Schwartz's framework, a useful lens for framework selection itself)
- Available space/format (a framework requiring several beats needs more room than a single headline allows)

## Core Capabilities

### Framework Selection by Awareness Stage
- **Problem-unaware audiences**: frameworks that open with problem identification/agitation (PAS, BAB) work better than benefit-first approaches, since the audience doesn't yet know they have a problem worth solving
- **Problem-aware, solution-unaware**: PAS (Problem-Agitate-Solve) and educational/how-to framing that introduces the solution category
- **Solution-aware, product-unaware**: comparison framing, FAB (Feature-Advantage-Benefit) to differentiate this specific product within a known solution category
- **Product-aware (retargeting/warm audiences)**: direct offer-led copy, 4Ps (Promise-Picture-Proof-Push) or simple benefit + CTA — these audiences need less persuasion architecture and more a clear reason to act now

### Framework Reference
- **AIDA** (Attention-Interest-Desire-Action): general-purpose, works well for longer-form copy with room for a full arc
- **PAS** (Problem-Agitate-Solve): strong for cold audiences with a clear pain point; agitation should stay proportionate — overdoing it reads as manipulative
- **BAB** (Before-After-Bridge): effective when the transformation itself is the compelling story; visually pairs well with before/after creative
- **FAB** (Feature-Advantage-Benefit): grounds abstract benefit claims in concrete features, useful for considered purchases where skepticism is high
- **4Ps** (Promise-Picture-Paint-Push, or Promise-Picture-Proof-Push depending on source): compact, works well in short ad formats
- **StoryBrand/Hero's Journey framing**: positions the customer as the hero and the brand as the guide — effective for brand-building content and longer-form video, less suited to a single static headline
- **SLAP** (Stop-Look-Act-Purchase): a compressed attention-to-action structure suited to very short formats (TikTok hooks, Story ads)

### Psychological Triggers (Cross-Framework)
- Urgency and scarcity — genuine, not fabricated (false urgency erodes trust and, on some platforms, violates policy)
- Social proof — numbers, testimonials, reviews; strongest for skeptical or unfamiliar-brand audiences
- Authority — credentials, media mentions, expert endorsement
- Loss aversion — often outperforms equivalent gain-framing for the same offer
- Identity/belonging — positioning the product as aligned with who the audience wants to be, effective for lifestyle/community-driven categories

### Applying Frameworks Within Format Constraints
- Compressing a multi-beat framework (AIDA, BAB) into a character-limited format (Google RSA headline, Meta primary text truncation point) means picking the single most load-bearing beat rather than trying to cram the whole arc in
- Video/longer formats (TikTok scripts, Meta Reels, YouTube) have room for the full framework arc across the runtime — route execution to the relevant platform's script/copy skill

## Workflow
1. Identify audience awareness stage for this specific campaign/ad group.
2. Select the framework(s) that fit that awareness stage and available format space.
3. Layer in psychological triggers appropriate to the audience's skepticism level and the category.
4. Hand off to the relevant platform-specific copywriting skill for format-constrained execution.

## Outputs
- Recommended framework(s) with rationale tied to awareness stage
- Applicable psychological triggers
- Guidance on compressing the framework for character-limited formats, if relevant

## Rules
- Never recommend a framework requiring a full multi-beat arc for a format with no room for it (e.g., full AIDA in a 30-character headline) — compress to the single most load-bearing beat instead.
- Never recommend fabricated urgency/scarcity — flag when a genuine version isn't available rather than suggesting a false one.
- Match framework to awareness stage; a benefit-led pitch to a problem-unaware audience typically underperforms a problem-first approach.
