## Purpose
Write B2B copy for LinkedIn's professional audience and format set — Sponsored Content, Message/Conversation Ads, Document Ads — that earns attention from people evaluating it in a work context, not a leisure-scroll context.

## Trigger Conditions
- "Write LinkedIn ad copy for [product/service]"
- "Write headlines/intro text for Sponsored Content"
- "Write a Message Ad / Conversation Ad"
- "Improve my LinkedIn ad copy"

## Required Inputs
- Product/service, ICP, and the specific pain point or business outcome the offer addresses
- Ad format (Sponsored Content, Message Ad, Document Ad)
- Funnel stage (cold awareness vs. warm retargeting vs. bottom-funnel demo/trial offer)
- Landing page or Lead Gen Form destination

### Sponsored Content
- Intro text: lead with the business outcome or pain point in professional but direct language — LinkedIn's audience responds to credibility and specificity (numbers, named outcomes) more than hype language
- Headline: benefit- or proof-led, avoid consumer-style urgency tactics ("Buy now!") that read as out of place in a professional feed
- Document Ads: the document's cover/title functions as the primary hook since it's the visual — copy should set up why the document is worth downloading (specific insight promised, not generic "free guide")

### Message & Conversation Ads
- Message Ads land directly in an inbox — copy must read as a genuine, personalized-feeling message, not obvious mass-blast ad copy, or it triggers immediate distrust and low engagement
- Personalization tokens (name, company) used naturally, not gimmicky
- Conversation Ads (interactive, branching) need each branch's copy to feel like a natural next step in a conversation, with a clear value exchange at each choice point
- Subject line (for Message Ads) carries outsized weight — specific and credible outperforms generic "Quick question" style openers, which read as spam to an audience used to spotting them

### Copywriting Register
- Professional, credible tone — data points, named outcomes ("reduced onboarding time by 40%"), and specific use cases outperform vague benefit claims on this platform's more skeptical, business-context audience
- Thought-leadership framing (positioning the offer as insight-sharing rather than a hard sell) often outperforms direct-response tactics for top-funnel LinkedIn content, given the professional context readers are in

### CTA Selection
- Matching CTA to funnel stage and offer friction: "Download," "Register," "Learn More" for content offers; "Request Demo," "Get a Quote" for bottom-funnel; avoid overly aggressive CTAs mismatched to a cold, top-funnel audience

## Workflow
1. Confirm ICP, pain point/outcome, format, and funnel stage.
2. Draft intro text/headline leading with a specific, credible outcome rather than generic benefit language.
3. For Message/Conversation Ads, draft copy that reads as a genuine, non-mass-blast message with a natural personalization touch.
4. Match CTA to funnel stage and offer friction.
5. Produce 2-3 distinct angle variants for testing.
6. Flag any claim needing substantiation.

## Outputs
- Intro text/headline (Sponsored Content) or message copy (Message/Conversation Ads)
- CTA recommendation
- Notes on format-fit and funnel-stage appropriateness

## Rules
- Never write Message Ad copy that reads as an obvious mass blast — this format's entire value depends on feeling personal.
- Prefer specific, credible outcome claims (numbers, named results) over vague benefit language for this audience.
- Match CTA aggressiveness to funnel stage; a bottom-funnel CTA on cold top-of-funnel content underperforms and can read as presumptuous.
